2.2

You read about a new study with the headline “eating caviar linked to longer lifespan.” The study’s research question is “does eating caviar make you live longer?” In the study’s data, they find that people who eat caviar have, on average, longer lifespans than people who don’t.

  1. What are some alternate explanations for this relationship?

    Maybe people who eat caviar are wealthier, and therefore have better medical care, better diets, healthier lifestyles, or safer jobs

  2. What sort of variation would identify the answer to the research question?

    A positive relationship between lifespan and controlled caviar consumption for a random representative sample of the population being referred to

  3. Give one suggestion for how the study authors might isolate variation that would identify the answer to the research question

    Use a random sample to control for income, geography, and other possible confounders

Why is a variable that causes both the “treatment” and “outcome” variables especially concerning for identification? You may want to use the phrase “alternate explanation” in your answer.

2.5

Shoe company Crikey claims that people who wear their fancy and expensive professional running-shoe Cool Mistrunner brand run 4 to 5% faster than if they wore an average shoe.

  1. In a few sentences, describe the data-generating process (you will probably leave some things out, that’s okay).

    Crikey stipulates that people who wear their shoe run faster because they are wearing those particular shoes. Also worth considering in the DGP are other factors which might make these users of the shoe run faster. People who are motivated and practice a lot generally become faster runners. People who are comfortable in their finances and can spend on hobbies may have more time to put into running as a sport. People who are healthier may be faster runners.

  2. What are possible alternative explanations for this claim, aside from the shoe making the person run faster?

    People who consider themselves to be runners may be more motivated to buy a specialized, expensive shoe, so they might be a faster runner regardless of the shoe. Also, people with a decent amount of money might be more capable / willing to buy the shoe because it is an expensive commodity, and since they are wealthier, they might be able to have a better diet, get better medical care, afford personal training, or are socially incentivized to play sports for work. All of these things could make them faster runners, and the shoe is just a signal of their means and interests.

  3. In running their study, the researchers accounted for some alternative explanations, including: gender, enthusiasm for running, and whether runners have participated in marathons and/or half marathons. Think of an alternative explanation not on this list. What is the implication of not accounting for this alternative explanation?

    Wealth, like I mentioned before. People who have enough money to buy the shoe have plenty of other reasons to have more time to put into the hobby and to be healthier in general. The implication of avoiding this is that they are not finding an effect of the shoe on running speed, but the impact of being a valid consumer of their product on running speed.
